Colonialism in the Works of Frantz Fanon

Black Skin, White Masks (1952)

Chara looms large in the background of Undertale. While they only appear in person once, their ghost haunts the entire game. This is not just meant metaphorically. Frisk, upon falling into the Underground, absorbed what remained of Chara’s soul. There are numerous signs that this is the case, but the most obvious one is that Chara’s name overrides Frisk’s for the majority of the game. This parallels the situation in Deltarune, where the protagonist is possessed by a foreign soul. But unlike Deltarune, in Undertale you control the vessel, not the possessing spirit. Furthermore, as Chara makes clear in their only appearance, the soul and determination were ‘yours’ all along. Whether that ‘you’ is the player or Frisk is unclear, but it shows that despite the parallel, Chara is not controlling Frisk. Nevertheless, their presence changes something. Frisk’s soul is red, like Chara’s. It is Chara who gains EXP, whose LOVE is increased. And in the end, that accumulation of EXP is what enables Chara to rematerialize as a separate entity in the genocide ending. Their Being consolidates the more you kill.

This seems to be a severe indictment of their character, and that is how it has largely been treated by players. Chara is the ultimate evil. They are the antithesis to the game’s morality. I am not here to dispute this, exactly. What I am here to do is interrogate what exactly that antithesis consists in, and problematize the dualism which the game sets up.

Reification

In a Neutral route, Chara and Frisk remain an unresolved collage forever. In Pacifist, Frisk come into their own as a separate character, while Chara disappears. In Genocide, Chara materializes, but Frisk is discarded. It seems, in other words, like they can only coexist if Frisk simply keeps going about their life without choosing to fully commit to or contradict what the game continually tells you: that you do not need to kill anyone. Otherwise Frisk and Chara appear mutually exclusive, a simplistic duality embodying peace and conflict, good and evil.

This perspective belies a strand of profound ambiguity which permeates the entire game. Undertale is very concerned with the concept of determination. This is on one hand a very trivial observation. The game tells you, over and over, that you are filled with determination; and as it turns out, determination is a physical, tangible thing, found even in the more ethereal souls of at least some monsters. Determination is gamified as what lets you save in the first place; your determination overriding Asriel’s is what enables looping back to where you once were, to do things differently.

On the other hand, determination is complicated by its moral emptiness. Due to its gamification, you engage with determination regardless of what route you take. In the Genocide route, too, determination drives you forward. This is equally true for real life; determination is purpose, spirit, the organization of life around a principle. But it doesn’t imply any particular principle. It’s choosing a side and sticking to it. This ambiguity is most directly expressed in Gerson’s explanation of the Prophecy: the Underground will go empty because everyone is liberated, or because everyone is killed.

Ambiguity complicates the simple dualism between Frisk and Chara, too. An important aspect of the Prophecy is, after all, that the Angel specifically returns to the Underground after seeing the Surface. Chara is the only character who fulfills that criterion; thus the Prophecy implies that they always play a role in the emptying of the Underground, even in Pacifist. It seems more likely that Chara only becomes “the demon that comes when people call its name;” they are not so inherently.

This is supported by what we concretely know about Chara’s life. They climbed Mt. Ebott for an unhappy reason, fell and were adopted by the Dreemurrs. They developed a strong bond with Asriel, while they seem to have been alienated from everyone else. Only Asriel mourns them in the present day, after all; everyone else only mourns Asriel. We also know Chara liked chocolate, had an obsession with knives, and berated Asriel for his sensitivity. They had a creepy smile and liked pulling mean pranks. Finally, they sought to help the monsters escape and get revenge on humanity, which they explicitly despised. By killing themself and fusing with Asriel, they wanted to kill six more humans and break the barrier. Asriel prevented them from doing so, and was killed.

None of this implies intent to kill all monsters, or even humans, let alone destroy the world itself. It rather implies unconsidered spite, a child’s vague desire for revenge against ‘humanity’ without consideration for what that would actually entail. Chara’s appearance in the Genocide ending, then, seems to represent a radicalization rather than a simple continuation of their past life. It is explicitly your choice which created them in this form, as an avatar of power; you gave them purpose. What is the parallel in Pacifist, then? When Asriel regains his original body, he finally recognizes that Frisk is not Chara. Later on, if you make your way back to the beginning of the game, he acknowledges that “Chara wasn't really the greatest person.” If in Genocide Chara’s spirit is reified as an archetype, Pacifist is an exorcism. Chara’s return was a magic trick, only holding power over us so long as we believed it to be true. Once we acknowledge its unreality, it vanishes. Chara becomes nothing more than an alienated, lonely, hateful child who died a long time ago. Their spirit is put to rest.

This spectrum between spiteful reification and redemptive concreteness takes place within a very specific framework. Frisk’s transformation in Genocide is framed as one of dehumanization; their movement becomes erratic, they are described as unsettling, dust-covered, and the characters recognize then as neither human nor monster. At the end of it all, you face Chara in a void. They only express a desire to destroy everything, absorb it to grow ever stronger, and abandon it once there is nothing left to feed their lust for power. If you return to Chara after letting them recreate the world, they berate you for your ‘perverted sentimentality,’ feelings which they do not understand. The love and hate which originally bound them to their sibling and themself, the alienated kindness which motivated them, is gone. Chara has become a void.

Chara draws on a lot of horror tropes. Most obviously they are a case of the creepy child character, combining signifiers of immaturity with signifiers of evil. They embody elements of the ‘sociopathic’ serial killer, too, in the way their emptiness is described by Asriel. They are an evil spirit possessing you, and more specifically a creepypasta spirit possessing your video game. In this analysis so far, I have implied, too, that they are a monster whose power comes from recognition as such, but which is banished when recognized as no more than a concept; while in reification they become a demon, summoned by calling its name, with which you, in that act, seal a pact where your soul is lost forever.

There is another trope, however, which places the unsettling ambiguity underlying the game’s treatment of Chara in stark relief: that of the failed resurrection, ‘coming back wrong.’ Unlike the amalgamates, who are portrayed as harmless and accepted by their families, Chara is an rather straightforward example of the trope; they are monstrous in personality as well as body. The demon is a perversion of Chara, but also in that perversion not really Chara. It is something else.

To be perfectly honest, this extreme collation of horror tropes is a little ridiculous. Furthermore, it’s illustrative of what demon Chara really is: Undertale’s moral antithesis. Now, irredeemable monsters who simply exist to be ‘evil’ are obviously normal in fiction. In Undertale, though, Chara stands out because there is literally no other character like them. Everyone else is treated as being being a concrete person who is ‘redeemable;’ Chara isn’t. In the interpretation I have presented here, Chara is only afforded redemption in absentia. It may be argued that this is because Chara is well and truly dead, and the dead must be put to rest and not haunt the living. What makes that perspective inadequate is that Asriel, too, is resurrected, but he is not precluded from redemption; on the contrary, he is the final lost soul to be saved, and it is his power which shatters the Barrier. The example of Asriel is also troubling because he, too, admits to having killed everyone, destroyed the world and reset everything. Demon Chara, the embodiment of the pursuit of power, growth, the manifestation of your determination to destroy – is not at all unique in either intent or action.

Thus the viciousness with which they are vilified appears uniquely cruel. Chara the person is completely absent from the game; all that appears is an avatar of evil which is supposed to be the outgrowth of that same tormented child. Their spirit is only allowed to rest as long as it is held at arm’s length, and the final conversation about them is entirely optional, to boot. This is ultimately what disturbs the neat picture of harmony in the Pacifist ending: despite only being another damaged, spiteful child, Chara is merely discarded to historical facticity, and never offered an ounce of understanding.

Moralism

Chara is uniquely positioned in the game because they embody the evil qualities of the player. Not only that; they are in fact the ultimate result of that evil. Broadly speaking there’s two perspectives on the negative consequences of moral transgression. One is a simplistic moralism: evil is metaphysically punished for going against the telos of the universe. Another approach is to view evil as its own punishment; it entails a cutting off from the world, it torments you psychologically and it changes how people treat you. Theoretically this is what happens in the Genocide route. As Sans explains, LOVE is a measure of your capacity to kill, your increasing detachment from others. The monsters find you off-putting and put up fierce resistance; while your mercilessness makes Asriel help you bypass puzzles. There is even an element of psychological torment in the description that “you felt your sins crawling on your back.” This is all behavioral.

However, the game doesn’t just let you perform your in-game genocide and sit with whatever feelings that may stir in you. You cannot exaggerate how strongly the game condemns you for playing the route. You are stated, as a matter of fact, to have ‘sins.’ You are claimed to have no friends, to have no fun, to never be happy. You are claimed to be a threat to humanity. It goes beyond simply having the characters treat another character as the scum of the earth. The game treats you, the player, as morally repugnant. Your actions are weighed and judged by Sans, who damages you with KARMA. In this regard the game very directly dabbles in a teleological worldview where evil inevitably gets punished for its transgressions. It’s not subtle.

The culmination of all of this is Chara’s resurrection and their literal destruction of the world. They are so evil that their presence forever corrupts any Pacifist endings you get afterwards. They even continue the game’s berating of you if you decide to not destroy the world, wish to restore it, or come back after its restoration. “Since when were you the one in control?” “You think you are above consequences.” “Another path would be better suited.” “You made your choice long ago.” The issue is that Chara is quite literally an unknown factor for any player who is unfamiliar with the Genocide ending. Their resurrection cannot be called an organic development, because there is no indication that Frisk knows they are bringing something into the world. Even at the end, your will, your determination, is still yours; you decide if you accept the destruction of the world or not. Chara as a world-destroying demon is rather a creation of the developer to mete out poetic justice. If you want so badly to destroy everything, why not just destroy the whole game and move on?

Why go through all of this effort to condemn something that you yourself enabled in your video game? In a sense the answer is obvious: the game presents freedom as a tool which can be misapplied. You can choose to put in the effort to resolve everything peacefully, or you can do everything in your power to tear the game world apart. This is the aforementioned ambiguity inherent in determination as such. But having characters moralize to you so incessantly, especially at a meta level, is not a compelling approach to this dilemma. Part of the appeal of art is that it lets you push experiences to their extremities and let that speak for itself. Instead Undertale is organized teleologically: you can do whatever you want, but you sure as hell can’t expect to do so without being told if it’s good or bad by the arbiters of morality.

Undertale’s moralism is inseparable from the previously mentioned framework of dehumanization which characterizes the Genocide route. As you become more murderous, other characters cease to perceive you as human. The irony is obvious: the game framed by a ‘humans vs. monsters’ narrative allocates innate goodness to monsters, while the human sheds their humanity as they prove to be capable of existence without kindness. The monsters are ‘more human’ than humans. This is a deeply humanist metaphysics; by which I mean it posits ‘humanity’ as something positive, with clearly identifiable psychological and behavioral traits. A human essence, against which all deviation entails a movement away from humanity.

Humanism, like any teleological worldview, is bunk. The very fact that there exist humans who do not correspond to ideal visions of what ‘humanity’ is, makes those visions useless except as tools of exclusion. The sacrifice of abnormality is inherent to any essentialism. On the fringes of what the humanist counts as ‘human,’ there must exist experiences which are understood to be monstrous, against which ‘the human’ is defined. Scapegoats embodying everything which ‘the good’ reject. Ursula K. Le Guin’s parable ‘The Ones Who Walk Away from Omelas’ powerfully illustrates the consequence of such frameworks by positing a utopian community whose happiness depends on a single child suffering in their place. All characters in the community are faced with the decision to either accept familiarity on these rotten foundations, or to leave for an uncertain future rejecting the principle of human sacrifice.

It goes without saying that Chara fits into this archetype as well. Their rejection undergirds the entire Pacifist ending. They can only ever appear as the embodiment of all your wrongdoings, deferring responsibility for your deeds away to the unrelated scapegoat of monstrosity. They lived an unhappy life alienated from almost everyone around them, and now in death they cannot find recognition, let alone respect, reduced to an abstraction for punishing player transgression against fictional characters. It equally goes without saying that this undermines the entire conceit that all conflicts in Undertale can be resolved without violence. Any peace which rests on structural violence against an Other is a phony peace.

Nihilism

There is one perspective where demon Chara makes more sense as an organic outcome. As a case of the failed resurrection trope, we would expect them to have been brought back by someone they loved. And indeed, this does seems to be the case. It is Asriel who refers to Frisk as Chara. It is he who sees an ‘emptiness’ inside you. It is he who decides to partner with you in taking everyone else down. The demon Chara is a concept invoked by Asriel; that of a companion who is like him, with whom he can share his fate. The emptiness, the moral void. This concept coalesces with Frisk’s genocidal intent, to produce a personification which grows beyond either’s intentions. The demon that consumes all. Nihilism, caricatured as pure negation of everything existing in the most literal sense.

Nihilism is perhaps the most prevalent ideological bogeyman in modern history. The concept that someone could approach the world from the perspective that there is no inherent meaning to anything, and therefore that everything is ultimately the same, that all premade categories are false, that morality is arbitrary – is terrifying to those who would believe that the world is organized around human principles. Nihilism is obviously antithetical to humanism. In context of the game this manifests as a conflict between those who approach it as a video game, and those who adhere to the concept of a human essence and, like the game itself, treats the characters like they are real.

This is the core problem with Undertale. If you load the game after a Pacifist ending, Asriel, too, moralizes to you as if all the characters are actually living out their real lives as people behind your save. You are the only thing that can stop their happiness. But this is nonsense. As Ludwig Wittgenstein puts it, a picture of a boiling pot only shows what it shows; it does not contain a material pot with actual contents that can be examined. The game characters only exist so long as they are loaded. When Asriel ‘talks to you,’ he is all that exists in the game. A set of pixels is talking to you as if concepts have feelings which you can destroy. This is the core dissonance at the game’s root: Chara is not a real person, but the game treats everyone else like they are. It treats the player like they have to seriously consider the morality of playing a video game as if it was real, but stops short of exactly one character.

To be a little more charitable, I don’t think Toby Fox actually believes that his characters are literally alive, or that we should treat them like people. Instead, I believe the game operates with a concept of spirit or aura; that there is an irreducible, intangible ‘essence’ to artistic experience which we destroy if we push our engagement with a work too far. Or, as Kris puts it to Susie in Deltarune ch. 3: “Your friendships are real.” Trying to wring every last piece of content from the game destroys those bonds. The issue with this perspective is that there is no way for the game to inherently become less interesting. Everything is experienced differently; there is no universal truth. What one person finds to be boring and lifeless, others may appreciate in countless different ways. Thus for the game’s morality to work, it has to talk down to you; it has to tell you how you are supposed to experience it, and that those experiences are sacred. But there is no way that the ‘breaking of bonds’ can be translated into something objectively fruitless. I have the capacity to find substantial experiences in killing every character as well as befriending them.

In fact, the player is arguably mechanically rewarded for pursuing the Genocide route. It contains two of the most compelling fights in the game, with Sans especially standing out as an iconic, groundbreaking challenge that in many ways defines the game’s legacy. On the other hand, the Asriel fight in Pacifist is undermined by being literally impossible to lose; it turns determination into something automatic rather than an effort. Ironically, then, Genocide requires much more determination to complete. This illustrates my earlier point that determination is morally empty. “Brave ones, foolish ones. Both walk not the middle road.”From this perspective, the true evil in Undertale is giving up, letting go of your principles, half-assing it. Doing a Normal route and leaving it at that. Approaching the game as if it happens to you, and you are not playing it. Acting as if the game isn’t telling you anything about how it can be played. There is an untaken path where the game touches more on the lines of adjacency between world-affirmation and world-denial against the simplistic continuation of ordinary existence.

Chara and Asriel, in fact, bear a striking resemblance to an archetype from another franchise: the witches in When They Cry. Like the witches, Chara and Asriel start out as normal people, but become monstrous perversions of their past selves, abstract universals, icons, through endless repetition. From the perspective of eternity, you are a stranger in everyday life balancing between absolute being and total negation; and with this position comes both crushing alienation and terrific power. Having honed your existence, your determination, to the point of insanity, you can force through any subjective world you desire; but at the same time, it requires tremendous effort to keep yourself from giving in to the impulse to gain power from hurting others rather than renewing yourself in an ongoing spiritual healing of the world. This nihilist knife-edge balance is what marks greatness of spirit, a transformation of consciousness along golden truths that transcend facts; it is what marks determination. And that ambiguity makes the exploration of absolute evil OR total negation extremely compelling.

Instead the game implies that the only reason anyone would play the Genocide route is because they just want to see what happens; ‘because they are bored.’ This is completely hollow; I did not play the Genocide route out of any idle curiosity or boredom. I did it because I wanted to understand what is going on in the game thematically, and I wanted to challenge myself with the most mechanically compelling fight Toby Fox has designed. To undermine the route’s ambiguity is to undersell the capacity of such stories to be interesting, expressive, emotional experiences in their own right.

I still love Undertale. It remains funny, charming and cathartic if it’s approached the way it’s intended: naively. But tracing and ripping up the seams of the narrative opens a darkness that the game would rather you not dive to deep into; and unfortunately it is in these dark places that I find its most difficultly compelling, beautiful lost threads dangling, abandoned for a simpler morality play.

There is nothing left for us here.
Let us erase this pointless world, and move on to the next.

Deltarune

It seems Toby Fox is sensitive to all the aforementioned issues, because Deltarune is, so far, staking out a very different course. First and foremost, Kris is clearly an echo of Chara. Not only do they fill the same social space as the strange, alienated adoptive Dreemurr child, they essentially look like an older version of Chara, wearing similar clothes, sporting similar hair and similar interests. Kris, however, is a fully formed, complex character who, far from being a rejected antithesis, arguably constitutes the game’s moral core. In fact, a central narrative thrust of the game regards the conflation of Kris and the foreign Soul possessing them. You are in control, but as I referenced earlier, you are not the protagonist; Kris is. This conflation is visibly a source of great pain, resentment and exhaustion for Kris; a depiction which feels very pointed. Their dehumanization does not originate in their transformation into an avatar of evil, but in their sense of disempowerment and alienation. They are uncomfortable with the existence of other humans, aspiring to monsterhood like everyone around them. Asriel has left them alone with no friends. A foreign object has taken them as its vessel. The light, which they were born from, provides no comfort; they only find it in that darkness where things become more indistinct.

In making a Chara expy the protagonist, Deltarune in fact centers the ambiguous line between destruction and redemption. The directions you can take Kris are not nearly so far removed from one another as Pacifist and Genocide. While the Weird route differs significantly from the Normal one at several points, it does not fundamentally warp reality the way Genocide does. Character interactions are more subtle, less universally hostile. Indeed, the Shadow Mantle sidequest in Chapter 3 acts as an indication that the Weird route is not only not condemned by the game, but offered up as an equally important, complementary approach to the narrative. As we know Kris will act on their own and tear out the Soul in times of need, each route only represents the boundaries within which they allow themself to be controlled. Neither is morally absolutist; regardless of how nice you are to Kris, you are still taking their freedom away; likewise they always remain party to the Knight’s conspiracy. The major difference, in fact, lies in the level of friction which exists between the cage and the caged, the level of strain which you put on Kris as a conscious puppet. I take it as a given that there is some sort of link between what happened to Dess, the promise Kris made, the Knight’s conspiracy, and Noelle’s ice magic. It is this transgression, this ‘breaking of the game,’ which seems to invoke deeply buried trauma, that derails everything. Kris is tormented by the actions you make them do, both to Berdly and, especially, Noelle, who becomes the target of esoterically cruel manipulation. They do their best to undo the harm which they have caused in their effort to work with the parasite inhabiting their boy. In other words, psychological rather than physical violence defines the Weird route. It demonstrates that power is honed much more through the domination of others than their destruction.

This is where we get to the crux of the matter: experiences and relationships in Deltarune are not treated as sacred, but malleable. There is no clearer illustration of this than the fact that Kris is a cheater. In cheeky references to ‘entering numbers knowingly,’ the metatextual engagement with the game’s more esoteric aspects are narratively integrated in a way that goes far beyond Asriel’s reference to people watching the Genocide route on YouTube. You are not moralized to; it’s a simple statement of fact. You are expected to ‘cheat.’ Another side to this is the Dark Worlds being a perspective shift, a manifestation of forceful personality – determination – which makes the mundane magical.

Dark Worlds are exactly that force I ascribed to witchcraft, the flowing out of self to animate everything as experienced. Dark Fountains blot out the socially constructed truth, the light, to replace it with a world weaved from the force of the singular, the isolated, what is cut off from connection and thus surrounded by utter Otherness. This is what we may call Evil, the flipside of an all-encompassing Love which spreads out to embrace everything. Rejecting the bright lights of a predictable future, a prepackaged life, it follows Gerson’s words: “Why don't we go between the lines? It's darker there.” But the further from the light you stray, the closer you get to such dangerous, unstable situations that risk triggering a break. This is where the Titans come in, the fear of the dark which only destroys Otherness. The Roaring is absolute privation, the complementary opposite of the Prophecy’s blinding light which equally kills all possible futures.

Deltarune is concerned with finding the golden mean between these opposing poles. It is an all-encompassing affirmation of freedom with the recognition that this involves pain, destruction, despair and uncomfortable truths. As Ramb offers in counterpoint to Undertale’s moralism: isn’t this dip into annihilation where you find the real fun? It’s this refusal of totalizing dichotomies, of neat categories, which makes me believe that, while the game is obviously not finished yet, we’re not going to see substantially different endings between the routes. In chapter 1, Susie and Ralsei present you with two opposing approaches to ambiguity: either your choices don’t matter, or they matter in how they change the spirit, rather than the facts, of your journey. You can still be heroic if heroism is not ontologically determined to succeed. If so, like in Umineko the game presents you with a ‘catbox’ where the outside is stable but the contents are endlessly variable along changes of perspective. The entire game is a rebellion against the fatalist, reductionist view presented by Gaster at the outset: “No one can choose who they are in this world.” The game embodies the experience of visceral refusal to let things end this way, because no matter what happens, your freedom to experience the world as you see it cannot be taken from you. As awkward as it may be, as incomplete, as hollow, if it is done with intent, without irony, it is substantial, it is real, it is magic; because no experience needs to justify itself with reference to an outside. It simply is.
