Circular Dialectics

In the Phenomenology of Spirit, Hegel tells the coming-of-age story of the world spirit through a series of modes of perception. After each exploration, he moves on to what he perceives to be the logical next form that consciousness takes. The original Higurashi is similar; it is set up as a series of spiritual exercises which, little by little, chip away at the reader, nudging them to think for themselves and change who they are. Higurashi contains recursive tragedies, but the tragedies are building blocks for a transcendence in the narrative, and potentially in the reader as well. Tragedy is not a refusal to see the positive, but an acceptance of the negative. It is through the tragedy of the time loop that we can overcome our stagnant paranoia and build bonds that ensure safety and happiness.

But that is just a story; it is not reality. Nothing says that the loop should lead into something different; on the contrary, it’s a loop. What if we never moved on? Saikoroshi touches on elements of this, by placing Rika in a ‘perfect’ world where she is the only anomaly. Her parents are still alive, but they don’t understand her. She has no friends to speak of, because she is a complete outsider. The dam is going to be built, and so Hinamizawa will disappear; the world is moving on, and she is refusing to move with it. The story situates Rika as something both more and less than a person, because she is not the 11-year-old child she appears to be. She is a fiction, a cartoon, an idea. She is a witch; a witch of alienation and destruction, not creation. She is endless, because she is becoming unchanging in her repetition.

Saikoroshi stands as a grimly compelling conclusion to the story exactly because it disturbs the neat picture of an idyllic 1983 summer where everyone is friends and all the problems are solved. It eschews its own answers with profound unease: what if you can fall back into the parts of yourself you thought you had moved beyond?

Gō/Sotsu takes this unease to its limits. It begins with another return: Rika has been sent back to 1983 once again, without understanding why. No matter what she does, she is forced to relive increasingly violent versions of her own past, nirvana-less regurgitation of the original story over and over, steadily eroding any sense of grounding until she simply gives up.

There is no foundation to reach. You are just falling. Past some point you can’t take it anymore. Because, when it all happens again, when the train returns to a stop you passed hours ago, it feels like nothing will ever change. Like change itself is leading you back to where you already were. The world and the self dissolve. You can think of nothing but severing the thread.

But there is not one witch; there are two. By daring to move beyond that sacred summer of 1983, Gō/Sotsu pushes further into the possibility of transcendence, but it rebounds. As it coalesces in Satoko’s performance of the Watanagashi ritual on Rika in their bedroom, the story disembowels itself. In its own entrails it finds abject truth: evils are themselves powerful forms of reality-warping magic which can ultimately outgrow the confines of everyday existence.

Resituating Satoko as a spiteful, recalcitrant, unrepentant villain, dedicated to bringing Rika down with her at all costs, to force a recognition of her suffering by inflicting suffering, inverts the original story’s moral about strength through friendship. What we get instead is a reverse coming-of-age story where the characters refuse to learn, refuse to go beyond, refuse to become satisfied. Their bond chafes; they do not improve each other, they make each other worse. Rika and Satoko’s relationship is turned from a tether to reality into transdimensional, codependent torture play. What we get is a downward spiral of hatred, where what is created through their bodies eventually breaks out of and bypasses all pretense at reality in pursuit of the meta-realm. The story becomes nothing more than a backdrop to their fight, a stage for a play without characters; all they can do is tear each other down, because they have made it so.

And so it goes until the two agree to depart. “We will meet again when something else cries.” The girls themselves, in their specificity, don’t matter anymore. They are not actors anymore; they are observers. It’s in the returning myth that the witches find a niche where they can be unchanging, callous, numb – but never bored.

If determinism is something I impose upon myself, why does it still happen to me when I want it to stop? Because I look to someone outside myself to save me? A handsome cop will not sweep me off my 11-year-old feet; my blonde high school girlfriend is going to torture me until I break and admit to liking the recurrence; liking the stagnation; liking the destruction of all that is comfortable. Gō/Sotsu gets that Higurashi is at its strongest when it is a schizophrenic nightmare. It recognizes that the most resonant feeling it evokes is that of hopelessness: the feeling that the world is stacked against you, that everyone hates you, that, deep down, you know you are responsible, but it’s incomprehensible how that can be. You have done something terribly wrong, and it’s broken the world beyond repair. Relationships that used to comfort you become sickening prisons. The gaze of the other turns derisive, caustic. The world smothers. When you can’t live with yourself, it always feels like you’ve been here before. Memories, hallucinations and perceptions bleed into each other until it becomes one big slurry. You can’t move on.

The power of tragedy lies in the absence of conclusion; it touches the fundamental truth that the world is beyond all categories, beyond all meaning and nonsense, beyond language. Anti-teleological storytelling requires us to recognize that logic, ambiguity and absurdity are just modes of seeing a world that we ultimately can’t grasp in itself. Because ‘the world’ that we perceive is on a continuum with our perception of it. It has no ontological reality; it is experience. Rationality can only go so far. There is so much more to life, so many ways of seeing, articulating, touching, feeling. Reality does not speak; it shows itself.

There is comfort in despair.
